Equity · Inclusion

Not Everyone Starts From the Same Place: Mattering and Identity

Mercurio is clear on a point that is easy to overlook: mattering is not distributed equally in workplaces. Who gets noticed, whose contributions are acknowledged, whose voice is heard in meetings, whose development is invested in — these are not random. They are shaped by the same patterns of privilege and marginalisation that shape broader society.

Drawing on Mercurio (2024)

A mattering culture requires more than telling people they matter. It also requires organisational conditions that allow people to feel seen, heard, valued, and needed — and those conditions are not experienced equally by everyone.

In veterinary academic medicine in the UK, this is particularly relevant. Our teams are increasingly international and diverse — yet the structures that determine who is visible, who is promoted, whose ideas are credited, and who is trusted with autonomy often reflect legacy hierarchies.

🌐

International staff

Navigating language, cultural norms around hierarchy, and unfamiliar institutional practices — while being expected to perform at the same level from day one.

👩‍⚕️

Women in clinical leadership

Research consistently shows women's contributions in team settings are more frequently interrupted, credited to others, or overlooked in the moment.

🔧

Technical and support staff

Whose essential work is often invisible by design — they make things run without being seen to make things run. Their absence would be felt; their presence is taken for granted.

Consider your last three instances of publicly recognising someone's contribution at work:

Who did you recognise? What are their roles, backgrounds, and identities — and does the pattern reflect your whole team?
Who wasn't recognised — and might have deserved to be?
Are there structural factors in your team that systematically make certain people more or less visible?

Belonging · Everyday Actions

The Difference Between Inclusion and Mattering

Organisations often pursue inclusion as a policy goal — and that is necessary. But Mercurio draws a crucial distinction: inclusion is about creating structural access; mattering is about the lived experience of significance once you are inside the structure. You can include someone while making them feel irrelevant. Inclusion is the floor; mattering is what makes the room worth being in.

🚪

Inclusion (structural access)

"Everyone is invited to the meeting." "We have a diversity policy." These are necessary but not sufficient conditions for people to feel they matter.

✨

Mattering (lived significance)

"Your specific perspective shaped how we approached this." "I sought your input because you've seen this from a different angle." Significance must be actively communicated.

🗣️

When voices get talked over

tap to explore

If someone's point is restated by another without credit — name it: "I think that's what Fatima was just saying — Fatima, do you want to expand?"

📄

Authorship and credit

tap to explore

Review your last paper, grant, or report. Who contributed significantly but whose name isn't there? Authorship decisions are mattering decisions.

🌍

Cultural humility in recognition

tap to explore

Public recognition is not culturally universal. Some colleagues find individual spotlighting uncomfortable. Ask: "I'd like to acknowledge your contribution — how would you prefer I do that?"

🎓

Students and mattering gaps

tap to explore

International students, first-generation students, students from non-traditional backgrounds — research shows these groups are most likely to feel academically invisible. The same effort and curiosity apply.

Reflect — tick each as you consider it

Think about the distinction between inclusion and mattering in your own context:

Can I identify someone who is structurally included in my team but who may not feel they genuinely matter to it?
Have I ever noticed a colleague's contribution being credited to someone else — and what did I do?
Is there a student or colleague from a different background whose experience of mattering I could check in on this week?

Scenario · Allyship in Action

The Idea That Became Someone Else's Idea

📖Scenario: Faculty Research Strategy Meeting

You are in a faculty research strategy meeting. Dr Kwame, a recently arrived early-career researcher from Ghana, has been quiet throughout — he has mentioned privately that he finds the meeting culture here "very different." During a fast-moving conversation about a new interdisciplinary cluster, Kwame raises his hand and proposes that the cluster explicitly partner with African veterinary schools to create a genuinely bidirectional knowledge exchange.

His proposal is met with polite nods, then the conversation continues without engaging it. Three minutes later, a senior professor says: "What if we thought about some kind of international partnership — with African institutions, say?" The room lights up. "Great idea," says the Chair. "Could you develop a proposal?" You are sitting next to Kwame, who says nothing and folds his notepad closed.

💬 What do you do in this moment?
A
This happens — ideas often land differently based on timing and how they're expressed. It's awkward but not worth intervening now; speak to the Chair afterwards.
B
Intervene immediately — clearly, calmly, and specifically: "I think Kwame proposed exactly this idea about three minutes ago — Kwame, would you like to be the one to develop that proposal?"
C
Check in with Kwame after the meeting to acknowledge what happened and offer support. The moment has passed; intervening now would be disruptive.
D
The professor may not have heard Kwame — it might have been an honest oversight. Assume good faith and let it go.
Yes — the moment requires an immediate, specific response. What Kwame experienced is "idea appropriation" — which disproportionately affects people with lower institutional status and those from underrepresented backgrounds. An immediate, calm, specific intervention — naming Kwame, naming the original idea, redirecting the opportunity — is the mattering-conscious response. It is not accusatory. It is witnessing. Waiting teaches Kwame that his voice carries no weight in this room.
Worth reconsidering. "Speaking to the Chair afterwards" is a deferred, private response to a public wrong. The mattering violation happened publicly; the acknowledgement also needs to be visible. Deferring also allows the record to stand — the Chair approved the senior professor's version, not Kwame's. That record matters for future credit.
Partly right — but incomplete. Checking in with Kwame privately is compassionate and important. But it does not correct the public record. An in-the-room intervention tells the room what happened. If you can only do one, do the in-the-room intervention first.
Worth reconsidering. Good faith is worth extending — it may well have been unconscious. But the effect on Kwame is identical whether the intent was conscious or not. "I think that was Kwame's idea" is not an accusation — it is a correction. It can be offered with warmth and still be said.
Final Reflection — tick to confirm engagement

Key Takeaway · Week 4

Mattering is not distributed equally. Those with less institutional power, those navigating cultural difference, those in lower-status roles — all are at greater risk of invisibility. A mattering-conscious team notices this asymmetry and actively works against it, in real time, in the room.
