From Transaction to Significance: What Leaders Actually Do

Mercurio distinguishes between two fundamentally different orientations a leader can hold towards the people they work with. He calls these the transactional mindset and the mattering mindset. The difference is not about personality — it is about deliberate practice.

"The mattering mindset asks one question before every interaction: Does this person know they matter to me, to this team, and to this work?"

— Zach Mercurio, The Power of Mattering

In a transactional mindset, people are valued primarily for what they produce. This mindset is not malicious — it is often the default in high-pressure environments like veterinary teaching hospitals and research universities. The mattering mindset holds a broader question: regardless of outcome, does this person know they are significant?

↔️

Transactional: "Good case today."

Outcome-tied recognition. The implicit message: you matter when things go well.

✨

Mattering: "I noticed how you handled that owner — that took real skill."

Specific, effort-based acknowledgement. The implicit message: I see you, not just the outcome.

↔️

Transactional: "Morning everyone."

Efficient, but anonymous. Nobody feels singled out as significant.

✨

Mattering: "Morning, Priya — how did that overnight case resolve?"

Personal, specific, curious. Communicates: I remember you beyond our last interaction.

How many of your acknowledgements were outcome-tied ("good job") versus effort-focused ("I noticed how you…")?

Communication · Daily Practice

The Four Signals That Communicate Mattering (Or Its Absence)

Mercurio identifies four specific communication behaviours — mattering signals — that, when consistently present, create cultures where people feel significant. They require no budget, no policy change, no special occasion. They require only deliberate attention.

Tap each card to explore what each signal looks and sounds like in your context.

👋

Signal 1: Greeting With Intention

tap to explore

Using someone's name, making eye contact, asking a genuine question. Not "morning everyone" but a personal acknowledgement: I see you, specifically.

🔎

Signal 2: Specific Recognition

tap to explore

Not "great work" but "I noticed how you patiently explained the diagnosis to that family." Specificity proves you were watching.

💡

Signal 3: Asking for Input

tap to explore

Genuinely soliciting someone's perspective — and acting on it, or explaining why you didn't. Asking signals: your thinking has value here.

🤝

Signal 4: Connecting Work to Purpose

tap to explore

Linking what someone does to why it matters — to patients, students, the team. "The data you collect makes the anaesthetic protocol safer for every animal we treat."

Why Specificity Matters So Much

Generic praise ("you're great," "well done team") is invisible. The brain processes non-specific praise similarly to silence — it provides no information about what was noticed. Specificity is the proof of attention.

Mercurio distils all four signals into a single practical framework you can use as a quick self-check after any interaction. He calls it NAN.

The NAN Framework · Mercurio (2024)
N
Noticed
Does this person know I see them — specifically, as an individual?
A
Affirmed
Does this person know their contribution is valued and their effort is recognised?
N
Needed
Does this person know their presence is irreplaceable — that the team genuinely needs them?

After any significant interaction with a colleague or student, ask yourself: did they leave feeling Noticed, Affirmed, and Needed?

Tick each signal as you reflect on your own practice

Honestly assess how consistently you use each of the four signals:

I greet colleagues by name with genuine attention — not a broadcast to the room.
When I recognise someone's work, I name something specific — not just "good job."
I can identify one person whose work I will connect explicitly to purpose this week.

What Would You Do? · Scenario

The Monday Meeting That Erases Everyone

Even leaders with genuinely good intentions can inadvertently undermine a sense of mattering through the way they structure everyday moments. This scenario explores a common pattern in academic and clinical team settings.

📖Scenario: Weekly Team Meeting

Professor Chen runs a weekly 30-minute team meeting for her research group of eleven people — three postdocs, four PhD students, two lab technicians, an administrator, and a visiting researcher. The meeting follows the same format every week: updates from the faculty, week's priorities, brief updates from each postdoc, then "any questions?" The technicians and administrator never speak unless asked a logistical question. The PhD students offer updates only when prompted. The visiting researcher has not spoken in the three weeks since arriving.

Over the past month, one postdoc has quietly begun applying for positions elsewhere. A technician mentioned she "just gets on with it." The visiting researcher looks forward to returning home.

💬 What is happening in this meeting, and what might change?
A
The structure is efficient and appropriate to the hierarchy. Not everyone needs to speak in every meeting.
B
The quieter team members should take more initiative — the opportunity to speak is always there.
C
The meeting structure itself systematically communicates who matters most. By consistently allocating voice only to postdocs, the meeting becomes a weekly signal of relative importance.
D
The solution is to extend the meeting to give everyone more time — the problem is structural capacity, not culture.
Yes — meeting architecture is culture made visible. Every recurring meeting teaches people who matters in this group. When the same voices are consistently heard and the same people consistently silent, the pattern becomes a statement. Small redesigns — directly naming the visiting researcher in an introduction, asking technicians to share one update, a rotating "thought of the week" — create radically different signals at no cost in time.
Worth reconsidering. Efficiency and mattering are not opposites. The question is not whether everyone needs to speak every week, but whether the structure systematically excludes certain voices. A meeting can be brief and designed so that different people's contributions are acknowledged and visible.
Worth reconsidering. Psychological safety research consistently shows that people do not take initiative in spaces where they have learned their voice is not actively invited. The visiting researcher may be navigating cultural, linguistic, and hierarchical unfamiliarity simultaneously. The onus is on the structure and the leader to create the invitation.
Worth reconsidering. Time is rarely the constraint. A 30-minute meeting redesigned to briefly name different team members' contributions would not require additional time. The issue is not duration — it is intentionality about whose contributions are made visible.

Key Takeaway · Week 2

Mattering is not communicated through grand gestures — it is communicated through the structure of everyday moments. Who is named. Whose voice is invited. Whose work is made visible. The mattering mindset asks us to audit these everyday structures with fresh eyes.
