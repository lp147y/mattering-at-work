Foundation · Core Concept

The Fundamental Human Need You May Have Overlooked

▶   Before you read — take 90 seconds to hear Zach Mercurio explain why mattering matters.

The Power of Mattering at Work
Zach Mercurio · Harvard Business Review · 1 min 25 sec · Opens on YouTube
↗

We spend a substantial part of our adult lives at work — often around one third of our time. For those of us in healthcare, life sciences, and academia, work is not merely a job — it is a vocation bound up with our sense of purpose, identity, and contribution. Yet many of us, at some point, have arrived at work and wondered quietly: Does anything of what I do make a difference? Does anyone notice?

"Mattering is the fundamental human need to feel significant — to know that we are noticed, that we are important to others, and that we make a difference."

— Zach Mercurio, The Power of Mattering (2024)

Psychologist Morris Rosenberg and colleagues helped establish the concept of mattering in the early 1980s, describing it as the feeling that we are significant to others — that we are noticed, important, cared about, and that our views count. Unlike confidence, which can feel individual, mattering is fundamentally relational. We do not matter in isolation; we matter to someone — within our families, teams, communities and organisations.

The Mattering Deficit at Work

Nearly 1 in 3 workers report feeling invisible, and almost 65% report feeling underappreciated. When people do not feel seen, heard, and valued, this is linked with disengagement, poorer wellbeing, and increased intention to leave.

Mattering — A Working Definition

Mattering is both a feeling and a fact. It is the experience of knowing you are noticed, relied upon, and that your existence makes a genuine difference to the people around you and to the work itself.

In clinical, research, and academic settings, we often focus outward — on patients, students, and scientific impact. This campaign invites us to also look inward and sideways: at the culture we collectively create, and whether every person in our learning and working community feels noticed, valued, and significant.

A Moment of Invisibility — The Cleaning Worker

Every morning, before the building opens to staff and patients, a cleaning worker arrives to prepare the clinical and teaching spaces. They check that floors are spotless, waste bins are ready, surfaces are disinfected to standard, and supplies are stocked. They know the day's schedule — which rooms are in use, what each space needs to be ready. After each session, they clean thoroughly, dispose of waste safely, restock supplies, and reset the spaces for the next team.

Throughout the day, they move through corridors and communal areas, keeping the environment safe, clear, and organised for staff, students, and patients. It's meticulous, essential work. Without it, clinical and teaching activities could not run safely — infection risks would rise, organisation would collapse, and staff would lose time managing what this person quietly handles.

Yet during team meetings, case discussions, and planning sessions, the cleaning worker is never mentioned. Successful clinics, smooth teaching days, the safety of the environment — all of these depend on their labour. But their contribution is invisible.

At the end of a long shift, the cleaning worker reflects: "I keep this place running. I know I do. But does anyone see it? Does anyone think of me as part of the team?"

This is the mattering deficit: essential work, genuine expertise, real care — but systematically unseen. When someone contributes meaningfully yet their contribution is never acknowledged or connected to shared outcomes, they slowly learn that they don't truly matter to the team.

The Science · Three Dimensions

Noticed, Affirmed, Needed: The Three Pillars of Mattering

Mercurio — building on Rosenberg's foundational work — describes mattering as resting on three distinct but interconnected pillars. He captures them in a simple, memorable framework: NAN. Understanding each pillar is essential, because we can inadvertently satisfy one whilst completely neglecting the others.

The NAN Framework · Mercurio (2024)
N
Noticed
Your presence is acknowledged. Your absence would be felt. You are not invisible in the room.
A
Affirmed
Your contribution is valued. Your effort is recognised — specifically, not generically.
N
Needed
Your presence is irreplaceable. The team genuinely depends on what only you bring.

These three pillars are not the same thing — and a person can experience one without the others. A colleague can be noticed (everyone knows who they are) but not affirmed (their work is never specifically acknowledged) and not feel needed (they believe their role is dispensable). All three must be present for someone to genuinely feel they matter.

Scenario · Application

Recognising Mattering Deficits: A Day in the Clinic

Mattering deficits — the gaps where people feel unseen or unimportant — often hide in plain sight. They rarely announce themselves dramatically. More often, they accumulate quietly through small, everyday omissions. This scenario explores one such moment.

📖Scenario: Monday Morning, Teaching Hospital

Jamie is a senior research technician who has worked in your department for three years. She is technically excellent, reliable, and genuinely committed to the team's work. This morning, a new postgraduate student joins the group for the first time.

During the formal introduction, the PI introduces the student to every academic staff member by name, role, and a brief personal comment. When he reaches Jamie's section, he says broadly: "And the technical team are over here — they'll show you where everything is." Jamie, who prepared a detailed orientation pack since last Friday, says nothing. She smiles. She shows the student where things are.

Later, Jamie's request to attend a national conference in her field is declined due to "budget constraints" — though a postdoctoral researcher has just returned from an international congress.

💬 What is happening here, and what might you do differently?
A
This is a normal workplace dynamic — nurses and clinicians have different roles and different development budgets. Jamie should raise it through HR if she's concerned.
B
The introduction erased Jamie's individuality and seniority. The budget decision compounded it. A leader who noticed would have named Jamie specifically, acknowledged her contribution, and advocated equally for her development.
C
Jamie should speak up more and advocate for herself — this is about her personal communication style rather than a systemic issue.
D
The conference refusal is a resourcing issue unrelated to mattering — these are two separate problems that shouldn't be linked.
Yes — this is the key insight. Both incidents, seemingly minor individually, communicate the same message: you are interchangeable, not individual. Jamie prepared for three days but was acknowledged as part of a faceless group. Her professional development was declined while others' was funded. A mattering-conscious leader names people specifically, acknowledges their expertise in front of others, and applies equitable scrutiny to development decisions across all roles. The cumulative message matters as much as any single incident.
Worth reconsidering. Framing this as "different roles have different norms" risks normalising invisible hierarchies. Mattering is a universal human need — it doesn't vary by job grade or contract type. The question is not whether HR should handle it, but whether we, as colleagues and leaders, are creating a culture where this should need to happen at all.
Worth reconsidering. Psychological safety research consistently shows that placing the burden of advocacy on the person being overlooked compounds the problem. Mercurio's work argues that mattering is a leadership responsibility, not an individual's personal project.
Worth reconsidering. While the two incidents are distinct, their effect on Jamie is cumulative. Mattering deficits are rarely delivered in one blow — they accumulate through patterns. The pattern here is consistent: Jamie's contributions and development are treated as lower priority. Separating the incidents misses the pattern.

Key Takeaway · Week 1

Mattering is not a reward for performance or seniority. It is a baseline human need that every person on your team deserves — through being genuinely noticed and genuinely valued. The first step is learning to see where the gaps are.
