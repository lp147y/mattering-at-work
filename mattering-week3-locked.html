Wellbeing · Science

The Hidden Cost of Feeling Invisible: Mattering and Burnout

Burnout in veterinary medicine is well documented and well recognised as a crisis. The profession consistently records some of the highest rates of psychological distress and compassion fatigue of any healthcare group. But the drivers of burnout are often framed narrowly: workload, clinical complexity, moral distress.

Mercurio argues that one of the most potent but underrecognised drivers of burnout is a sustained deficit in mattering. He describes it plainly: feeling invisible is exhausting.

"When people don't feel they matter, they expend enormous psychological energy in its pursuit — monitoring for signs of significance, managing the emotional toll of invisibility, and questioning the value of their effort."

— Zach Mercurio, The Power of Mattering

This does not mean mattering is a cure for burnout — systemic causes require systemic solutions. But within the systems we inhabit, we hold significant power to either deplete or replenish the people around us through genuine recognition.

Protective Factors · Neuroscience

Mattering as a Protective Buffer: What the Research Shows

Neuroscience has illuminated something most of us sense intuitively: social belonging and significance activate reward circuits in the brain. When we feel we matter, the brain's threat-detection systems are less activated — we experience less chronic stress and are more capable of the complex empathic tasks that define veterinary work.

Conversely, social exclusion and invisibility activate the same neural pathways as physical pain. Feeling that you don't matter is not metaphorically painful — it is neurologically painful.

🧬

When mattering is present

Reduced cortisol response. Greater psychological flexibility. More capacity for empathy. Stronger intrinsic motivation. Improved error recovery.

⚡

When mattering is absent

Heightened threat vigilance. Increased cognitive load. Reduced empathy availability. Withdrawal and disengagement. Increased sick leave.

For anaesthesia and critical care specifically — where sustained attention, calm under pressure, and effective team communication are non-negotiable — the psychological cost of invisibility is not merely a wellbeing concern. It is a patient safety concern.

Scenario · Application

The Colleague Who Stopped Asking Questions

📖Scenario: Anaesthesia Department, Six Months In

Dr Aisha joined the anaesthesia team six months ago. In her first weeks she was engaged, asked many questions during ward rounds, and seemed genuinely enthusiastic. Over the past two months something has shifted. She is professional and competent, but quieter. She no longer asks questions during rounds. She completes her cases and leaves promptly. At the last team social event she made her excuses after thirty minutes. A colleague mentioned she recently declined an invitation to co-author a case report she had been instrumental in.

When you mention casually that you've noticed she seems quieter lately, she says: "I'm fine — just focusing on getting through the caseload."

💬 What might be happening — and what, if anything, do you do?
A
She has told you she's fine. Respecting her answer and not pressing further is the appropriate response — everyone goes through quieter phases.
B
The pattern of withdrawal — reduced engagement, social avoidance, declining a meaningful professional opportunity — warrants a more deliberate conversation. Find a private moment, name what you have observed specifically, and ask genuinely how she is experiencing the team.
C
This sounds like early burnout — refer her to occupational health and let the formal system manage it.
D
Invite her to coffee with the wider team — a social reconnection might help without the awkwardness of a formal conversation.
This is the mattering-conscious response. The pattern — withdrawal, declining meaningful opportunities, social avoidance after initial engagement — signals that something has changed in how Dr Aisha experiences her place in the team. A generic "how are you?" gets "fine." A deliberate, specific conversation — "I noticed you didn't take up the case report — that seemed unlike you, and I wanted to check in properly" — opens a different door. Witnessing her specifically is itself a mattering signal.
Worth reconsidering. "I'm fine" in the context of visible behavioural change is rarely the whole truth — it is often a social script. The pattern of withdrawal is more informative than the verbal reassurance. Taking it at face value can communicate: I won't push through your defences to actually see you.
Worth reconsidering. Occupational health is valuable — but escalating formally before having a genuine human conversation first risks communicating that her distress is a problem to be managed rather than a person to be seen. The most powerful first intervention is a colleague who notices and cares enough to ask specifically.
Partly right, but incomplete. Social inclusion matters — but group events are unlikely to address what has shifted for Dr Aisha. What is needed is an individual moment of genuine, specific noticing. The social invitation can complement but not substitute a real conversation.

Key Takeaway · Week 3

Burnout is not just about workload — it is also about significance. When people feel invisible, they expend psychological resources managing that invisibility. Our attention is a resource. Directing it specifically at the people around us is one of the most protective things we can do.
