Culture · Systems Thinking

From Individual Acts to Institutional Culture: The Architecture of Mattering

Over the past four weeks we have focused largely on interpersonal mattering — the daily signals exchanged between individuals. But Mercurio argues that the goal is something larger: an institutional culture where mattering is built into processes, structures, and norms — not dependent on the generosity of individual leaders.

Campaign Principle

Mattering becomes fragile when it relies only on individual goodwill; it becomes durable when it is embedded in organisational systems, routines and culture.

Mercurio identifies what he calls mattering infrastructure — the institutional practices that systematically communicate significance to every member of the community.

📋

Structured recognition systems

Regular, role-diverse recognition — not just clinical achievement awards. Technicians, administrators, students whose work enables others. Named, specific, public.

🔄

Feedback loops that close

When someone raises a concern or suggestion, the system responds — even if the answer is "we heard you, and here's why we can't act on it." Silence erases.

🎯

Purpose-linking onboarding

New members — at every level — are explicitly told how their specific role connects to the broader mission from day one. Not assumed. Stated.

🤝

Cross-role visibility events

Structured opportunities for different parts of the team to understand each other's work — so that dependency is visible, not invisible.

📊

Equity audits of recognition

Periodically reviewing who gets nominated, credited, promoted, and invited to lead — and whether the pattern reflects the team's actual composition.

💡

Protected development for all roles

Training, conference attendance, and professional development available equitably — not just to those in senior positions.

Team Audit — tick each as you reflect

Look at the list above. In your team or department:

Is there a formal mechanism for cross-role recognition that reaches everyone — or only those at certain grades?
When a concern is raised through a formal channel, does the person who raised it hear back? What happens then?
If a new technician or administrator joins tomorrow, who explicitly tells them why their role matters to the team's mission?

Practical Skills · Communication

How to Tell Someone They Matter — Specifically

Most of us are not taught to communicate significance deliberately. We absorb cultural norms — British reserve, professional stoicism, clinical detachment — that can make explicit acknowledgement feel awkward or even unprofessional. Mercurio's argument is that this is a skill, and skills can be practised.

He offers a simple but powerful framework for the "mattering conversation" — a brief, intentional moment that communicates significance. It has three elements:

1️⃣

Name what you specifically noticed

Not "you did well" but "I noticed you stayed with that family for an extra twenty minutes after the procedure — she needed that." Specificity proves attention.

2️⃣

Explain the impact

Not "good job" but "it meant that family left feeling cared for rather than processed — and that matters for everything we're trying to build here." Connect the action to something larger.

3️⃣

Express that their presence is not interchangeable

Not "the team is great" but "I'm genuinely glad you're on this team — the way you approach things changes the room." Make the irreplaceability explicit.

Campaign Principle

Mattering does not always require grand gestures. Sometimes a ninety-second conversation can change how someone carries the next ninety days.

Practice Commitments — tick what you will do this week

Choose at least one of the following to practise this week:

I will find one colleague and say something specific I noticed — naming what I saw and why it mattered.
I will send one short, specific email or message — not in response to a request, just naming something I noticed.
At my next team meeting, I will begin with a one-minute specific recognition: naming one person and one specific thing I observed.

Scenario · Appraisal

The Appraisal That Said Everything and Nothing

📖Scenario: Annual Appraisal, Research Department

Dr Mei has been a senior research associate for four years. She is highly competent, publishes regularly, and is well respected by the postgraduate students she supervises. In her annual appraisal, her line manager tells her she is performing at the expected level, that her research is on track, and that there are "no concerns." The conversation lasts twenty-two minutes.

Mei leaves the appraisal feeling flat. She could not say exactly why — nothing negative was said. She walks back to her office and begins updating her CV.

💬 What went wrong — and what would a mattering-conscious appraisal look like?
A
The appraisal did its job — it assessed performance and identified no concerns. Mei's emotional response is about her expectations, not the appraisal.
B
The manager should have set clearer development goals — the problem is the absence of a growth plan, not a mattering issue.
C
The appraisal communicated competence but not significance. Mei knows she is adequate — she does not know she is valued. A mattering-conscious appraisal names the specific, irreplaceable contribution she makes and expresses genuine investment in her future here.
D
Mei should have raised the things she wanted to discuss — the appraisal is a two-way conversation and she has agency to steer it.
Yes — performance assessment is not the same as mattering. The appraisal told Mei she is meeting expectations. It did not tell her she is significant, valued, or irreplaceable. "No concerns" is not a mattering statement — it is an absence of a problem. A mattering-conscious appraisal would include: a specific, named acknowledgement of her contribution; a genuine inquiry into how she is experiencing the work and the team; and an explicit expression of investment in her development and future here. The CV update tells us something has been consistently missing.
Worth reconsidering. Mei did not leave expecting a prize — she left with no information about whether she matters to the people she works alongside. That is not a misaligned expectation; it is a genuine unmet need. The appraisal system as described is functional but not mattering-conscious.
Partly right — but incomplete. A growth plan would help. But Mei's flatness is not primarily about the absence of goals — it is about the absence of being seen. You can have a detailed development plan and still leave feeling interchangeable.
Worth reconsidering. The structure and culture of appraisal determines what feels speakable. If the room communicates "this is a performance assessment," raising questions about significance feels out of place. The responsibility for creating conditions where mattering can be communicated rests primarily with the person holding the power in that room.

Key Takeaway · Week 5

Culture is the sum of what we normalise. When we integrate mattering signals into institutional processes — appraisals, meetings, recognition systems, onboarding — we shift from depending on individual generosity to building collective infrastructure. That is what makes mattering durable.
